Written at age 30 (part 2) - Progress Report in Apr, 2023

Posted on 2023-04-03 Edited on 2023-05-14 In BiWeeklyUpdate

This year, I’ve experienced some changes in my mindset.

The biggest and smallest change is that I willingly accept any opinions, criticisms, complaints, or even disappointments from others about me.

For example, one day, my friend on WeChat expressed her disappointment in me: “After so many years, you should have been praised and recognized by people in your hometown. You are not as outstanding as we had imagined, and even very mediocre.” My first reaction was to admit the truth. I am indeed very mediocre, and often work very hard but don’t achieve good results.

However, if those people predicted me wrongly, it should be their judgment problem, not my problem. Following this premise, I found that after accepting any opinions from others, I didn’t feel the need to prove myself so much anymore. The benefit of this is that I can finally slow down and not rush to succeed. My mantra now is “not too fast, not too slow,” and I repeat it to myself before doing anything. In the past, I was too eager to make quick profits. During the bull market in the cryptocurrency market, I helped the project team send two tweets, which immediately brought in 300 USDC. I felt like I was surrounded by money, and I even wanted to write “cryptocurrency expert” on my face. However, the fact is that although I have earned a bit through smart contracts in recent years, it’s only a little. Every weekend, I was busy writing various arbitrage strategies, but so far, I have lost more than I have earned, which is really embarrassing.

Like Xu Zhisheng said, I can accept that I won’t be successful next year or even in the next three years, but I cannot accept that I won’t be successful in the next five years or even in my lifetime. I am more tolerant of myself now. I can accept that I won’t make any progress in the next three to five years, still a beginner in Solidity programming language. However, I don’t allow myself to be mediocre for ten years or for my whole life.

Following this logic, as I slow down, my emotions become much more stable. I used to be loud and get excited when something good happened, but I panicked when I faced difficulties. Recently, I have become much more stable. There is no need to be happy just because Bitcoin has gone up. If that’s the only reason for happiness, then you won’t be able to sleep when it goes down. But in fact, whether it goes up or down, it won’t affect how good or bad you eat for dinner. And as for my Bitcoins, unless there is a moment when I need to change my life with money, I probably won’t sell them in my lifetime. Just owning them is enough to make me happy, and I’m not kidding.
